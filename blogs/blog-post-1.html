Equilibrium Strategies and Expected Payoffs in the Continuous Yankee Swap Mechanism

The following research report provides an exhaustive game-theoretic analysis of the continuous sequential allocation game—commonly analogous to a "Yankee Swap" or "White Elephant" mechanism. In this formalized setting, 101 perfectly rational actors participate sequentially to maximize their individual expected payoffs. The assets (boxes) contain hidden values independently drawn from a continuous uniform distribution on the interval $[0, 1]$.

Unlike discrete versions of the game, this continuous formulation permits mathematically unbounded stealing. The structural mechanics dictate that when a player's box is stolen, that victim is instantaneously compensated by drawing a new box from the $U(0,1)$ distribution. The rigorous analysis reveals profound insights into backward induction, subgame perfect Nash equilibria (SPNE), and the deeply counterintuitive nature of optimal stealing heuristics.

1. Axiomatic Foundations and State Space Mechanics

To derive the subgame perfect equilibrium, it is first necessary to rigorously define the state space of the continuous game and establish the invariant properties of the probability distributions in play.

Let $N$ be the total number of players. The game proceeds sequentially over $N$ discrete turns. Because rational decision-making in finite sequential games relies on backward induction, it is mathematically more functional to index the game by the number of turns remaining, denoted as $k$. Thus, the first player acts at $k=N$, and the final player acts at $k=1$.

At the onset of any turn $k$, there are exactly $N-k$ boxes that have been previously opened. The player acting at turn $k$ is presented with two mutually exclusive strategic options:

  1. Steal an exposed box: The player selects one of the $N-k$ visible, exposed boxes. The victim of this theft is subsequently forced to draw a new replacement box from the $U(0,1)$ distribution.
  2. Draw a new box: The player bypasses the exposed boxes and draws a newly generated box from the $U(0,1)$ distribution.

1.1 The Invariance of the Random Variable Pool

A critical property of this mechanic is the strict preservation of the independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) nature of the exposed boxes. Regardless of whether a player chooses to steal an existing box or draw a new one, exactly one new box is introduced into the game's ecosystem during that turn. The specific sequence of thefts does not alter the underlying statistical distribution of the boxes in play; it solely determines the permutation of ownership.

2. Backward Induction and the Drawing Threshold Sequence

Since the game features finite sequential moves with perfect information, it is solved analytically using backward induction. Let the expected payoff for a player with $k$ turns remaining be denoted as the threshold value $t_k$.

2.1 The Terminal Player ($k=1$)

The final player (Player 101) faces 100 exposed boxes. Let the maximum available exposed box be denoted by the first order statistic, $M = X_{(1)}$. The expected value of a newly drawn box is the standard mean of the $U(0,1)$ distribution, which is $1/2$.

Therefore, the baseline expected value for drawing at $k=1$ is:

$$ t_1 = \frac{1}{2} $$

Player 101 will deterministically steal $M$ if $M \ge 1/2$; otherwise, they will draw a new box.

2.2 The Penultimate Player ($k=2$)

The player with two turns remaining must anticipate the final player's greed. If the player with $k=2$ draws a box $x$:

  • If $x \ge t_1$, the final player will steal it, and the $k=2$ player gets a replacement draw with $k=1$ remaining, worth $t_1$.
  • If $x < t_1$, the final player will not steal it, and the $k=2$ player keeps $x$.

The expected payoff $t_2$ is:

$$ t_2 = \int_0^{t_1} x \, dx + \int_{t_1}^1 t_1 \, dx = t_1 - \frac{t_1^2}{2} $$

Given $t_1 = 0.5$, we find $t_2 = 0.5 - 0.125 = 0.375$.

2.3 The General Recurrence Relation

This yields the fundamental recurrence relation governing the game's drawing thresholds:

$$ t_{k+1} = t_k - \frac{t_k^2}{2} $$

3. Order Statistics and The Yield Calculus of Stealing

A naive interpretation suggests that a rational player should reflexively steal the absolute highest exposed box, $M$. However, rigorous analysis proves this heuristic to be a severe mathematical trap for early and mid-game actors.

3.1 The "Sacrificial Anode" and the Penalty of Visibility

When a player steals a high-value box, it remains visible to subsequent actors. The highest boxes act as "lightning rods," absorbing the deterministic greedy thefts of the final players. If you steal the $r$-th highest box, you will hold it until it is stolen by the player acting with $r$ turns remaining. Your replacement draw will then face $r-1$ subsequent players, yielding an expected value of $t_{r-1}$.

// The Yield Paradox

Stealing the absolute best box is often the worst possible optimal steal for early players, as it guarantees they will be stolen from by the very next player.

4. The Master Subgame Perfect Strategy Formulation

A rational player acting with $k$ turns remaining must evaluate three avenues:

  1. The Safe Harbor Yield: Steal the $k$-th highest box. Because there are only $k-1$ players remaining to act, they will at most consume the $k-1$ boxes ranked higher than yours. You keep the $k$-th box permanently.
  2. The Shield Box Yield: Steal a lower-ranked box that is still $> 1/2$. This ensures you are stolen from as late as possible (by the final player), giving you a replacement draw worth $t_0 = 1/2$.
  3. The Baseline Draw: If no exposed box exceeds the threshold $t_k$, draw a new box.

5. Conclusion

The 101 boxes game is an immaculate statistical sorting algorithm. The $k$-th player from the end is functionally guaranteed to receive the $k$-th highest realized value from the global pool. The mechanism highlights the severe positional disadvantage of acting early; inaugural actors are systematically penalized by recursive expropriation, relegated to expected payoffs approaching absolute zero, while terminal players capture near-maximum utility.
